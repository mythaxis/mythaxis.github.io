Editorial

What did I say last time out? That it’s time to wind down the editorialising in favour of the things that really matter, like enjoying great creative writing and maybe even doing a bit more of my own? Yeah, I think that about sums it up.

On the subject of the first, we do indeed have six great new stories. As well as delivering your crime neat they also mix it with comedy, horror and science fiction, including pieces from one returning contributor and one debutant to the fiction scene. Plus we of course have another longform non-fiction essay and a cluster of bite-sized recommendations from amongst the crime shorts I’ve read elsewhere online.

In fact, 2025 has predominantly been about crime reading for me. Of the nearly 70 books I’ll have finished come the end of the year, 40 were crime novels of one variety or another, two were collections of classic shorts (Dashiell Hammett’s The Continental Op and Raffles: The Amateur Cracksman by E. W. Hornung, this last pair contemporaries of Sherlock Holmes and Arthur Conan-Doyle respectively), and one a novella (Leystone by Stephen Volk, whom I intend to read much more of in 2026). I won’t go into detail about the novels here, because several will feature in my largely defunct blog’s single annual post presenting my top ten reads, and I wouldn’t want to spoil that.

This shift from reading mostly speculative fiction is no coincidence, as the uptick in my crime reading over the last few years has coincided with an increasing urge to venture out with my writing into the genre. In my previous crime issue editorial, I didn’t so much swallow my pride as vomit it forth due to the sale of my first short crime story – a tale of drug dealing at a late-90s rural disco called The Ends, now available in the Motives Unknown anthology. And it’s a nice collection, my pride only increased after I had a chance to read the pieces it was buried with. You should go support Dead Ink Books and buy a copy!

Since then, I’ve been planning a novel spinning off characters from The Ends, strongly inspired by the writing of Derek Raymond, from whose Factory series came one of my favourite books of the year. I also finished the first draft of a short genre-straddling fantasy/action/legal thriller novel, now undergoing revision into a not-so-short version of the same. If you’re reading these words before New Year’s Eve, there’s a pretty good chance I’m working on draft two as you finish this sentence.

If you’re reading these words after New Year’s Eve… let’s be honest, I’m probably still working on it.

Anyway, that’s enough for this supposed-to-be-vestigial editorial. Forgive me as I take a final moment of your time in order to thank: you, for reading our stories; our contributors, for writing them; Micah Hyatt, for his fantastic audio production efforts; and Marty Steer, without whose work behind the scenes Mythaxis would long since have become a nostalgic memory.

I wish you all a Happy New Year!

Andrew Leon Hudson

Andrew is a technical writer by day, and is technically a writer by night as well. In addition to editing Mythaxis he has been published in a small handful of quality zines, and co-authored a serialised alternate history adventure novel. He lives in Barcelona, Spain, and doesn’t do things online often enough to count.
